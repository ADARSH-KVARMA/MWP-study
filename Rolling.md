  - A cold-rolling method.

## ðŸ”§ Types of Rolling Mills

1. **Two-High Reversing Mill (Fig. 17-1b)**  
   - Rolls rotate in both directions.  
   - Workpiece can be passed back and forth without manual return.  
   - Improves productivity compared to a simple two-high mill.

2. **Three-High Mill (Fig. 17-1c)**  
   - Upper and lower rolls are driven.  
   - Middle roll rotates by friction.  
   - Workpiece can be passed alternately through upper and middle, then middle and lower rolls â†’ no need to reverse roll direction.

3. **Four-High Mill (Fig. 17-1d)**  
   - Uses **small-diameter work rolls** for reduced power requirements and precise rolling.  
   - Small rolls lack rigidity, so they are supported by **large backup rolls**.  
   - Enables rolling of **very thin sheet** to close tolerances.

4. **Cluster Mill (Fig. 17-1e)**  
   - Each work roll is supported by **two backing rolls**.  
   - Provides extra rigidity for rolling thin sheet.  
   - **Sendzimir Mill**: a modification of the cluster mill, specialized for rolling **thin sheet or foil from high-strength alloys**.

## ðŸ­ Tandem Mills (Fig. 17-2)
- For **high production**, multiple rolling mills are installed in series.  
- Each set of rolls = a **stand**.  
- Each stand takes a different reduction, so strip velocity changes at each stage.  
- **Synchronization**: Roll speeds are carefully controlled so each stand matches the delivery speed of the previous one.  
- This allows continuous, efficient rolling of long strips.

### Comparison of Hot Rolling vs. Cold Rolling

| Feature | Hot Rolling | Cold Rolling |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Temperature** | Performed above the recrystallization temperature (typically $> 0.6 T_m$). | Performed below the recrystallization temperature (usually at room temperature). |
| **Strain Hardening** | Strain hardening is rapidly eliminated by dynamic recovery and recrystallization; the metal does not retain the hardening. | Strain hardening (work hardening) occurs; strength and hardness increase while ductility decreases with deformation. |
| **Flow Stress & Force** | Flow stress is low and essentially constant. Lower energy and forces are required for deformation. | Flow stress increases with deformation. High rolling forces and power are required. |
| **Microstructure** | Distorted grain structure is replaced by new, equiaxed, strain-free grains. Cast structures (dendrites/porosity) are broken down and refined. | Grains are elongated in the direction of rolling. A strong crystallographic texture (preferred orientation) is developed. |
| **Surface Finish** | Poorer surface finish due to high-temperature oxidation and scale formation. | Superior surface finish. |
| **Dimensional Control** | Tolerances are wider due to thermal expansion/contraction and the presence of scale. | Close dimensional tolerances can be maintained; produces precise gauge thickness. |
| **Friction** | High friction coefficients ($\mu \approx 0.2$ up to sticking). | Low friction coefficients ($\mu \approx 0.05 - 0.10$) with effective lubrication. |
| **Minimum Gauge** | Limited; difficult to roll very thin sections due to heat loss and roll flattening issues. | Capable of producing very thin sheet and foil (especially on cluster/Sendzimir mills). |
| **Typical Applications** | Initial breakdown of ingots into blooms, billets, and slabs; production of rails, structural shapes, and thick plates. | Production of sheet, strip, and foil; finishing operations to achieve specific mechanical properties (tempers). |

Based on **Chapter 17, Section 17-8 (Problems and Defects in Rolled Products)** of the provided text, rolling defects can be categorized into problems concerning dimensional accuracy (gauge), shape (flatness), and structural integrity (cracking).

Here is a detailed explanation of these defects:

### 1. Dimensional and Gauge Defects
These defects arise from the elastic deformation of the rolling mill itself. Because rolling forces are high ($1 \text{ to } 3 \text{ GN m}^{-1}$), the mill is not a rigid body.

*   **Mill Spring:** Under high rolling loads, the rolls flatten and bend, and the housing stretches. Consequently, the **exit thickness** of the sheet is always greater than the roll gap setting under no-load conditions. To control this, the mill's elastic constant must be known and compensated for.
*   **Limiting Gauge (Minimum Thickness):** There is a limit to how thin a sheet can be rolled. As the sheet gets thinner, the resistance to deformation increases. Eventually, the rolls flatten elastically rather than plastically deforming the workpiece. The limiting thickness ($h_{min}$) is proportional to the coefficient of friction ($\mu$), roll radius ($R$), and flow stress of the metal ($\sigma_0'$), as given by the formula:
    $$h_{min} \approx \frac{\mu R \sigma_0'}{12.8}$$
    *(for steel rolls)*

### 2. Shape and Flatness Defects
"Shape" in rolling terminology refers to flatness. Defects here are caused by **roll bending** along their length. If the roll gap is not perfectly parallel, one area of the sheet will be reduced more than another. Since volume is constant, the area with greater reduction must elongate more.

*   **Wavy Edges (Long Edges):**
    *   *Cause:* The rolling force tends to bend the rolls apart at the center. If the rolls deflect, the edges of the sheet are compressed more (thinned more) than the center.
    *   *Result:* The edges elongate more than the center. Because the sheet is a continuous body, the edges are restrained by the center and buckle into waves (Fig. 17-9d).
*   **Center Buckles (Loose Middle):**
    *   *Cause:* This is the opposite of wavy edges. It usually occurs if the rolls have too much **camber** (crown) ground into them to compensate for deflection. The center is thinned and elongated more than the edges.
    *   *Result:* The center fibers buckle because they are longer than the edge fibers.
*   **Zipper Breaks:**
    *   *Cause:* Under certain conditions of differential elongation (center stretching more than edges), the tension in the center may cause cracks along the centerline of the sheet (Fig. 17-9e).

### 3. Cracking and Fracture Defects
These defects typically result from **inhomogeneous deformation** and secondary tensile stresses generated during rolling.

*   **Edge Cracking:**
    *   *Cause 1 (Lateral Spread):* As metal passes through the rolls, friction opposes lateral spread more at the center than at the edges. This uneven spreading puts the edges in tension, leading to cracking (Fig. 17-10b).
    *   *Cause 2 (Inhomogeneous Deformation):*
        *   **Light Reductions:** Only the surface is deformed. The overhanging material is not compressed directly and is forced to elongate by the center, setting up secondary tensile stresses at the edges (Fig. 17-11a).
        *   **Heavy Reductions:** The center expands laterally more than the surface (barreling), creating secondary tensile stresses that crack the edges (Fig. 17-11b).
*   **Center Splitting:**
    *   *Cause:* Extreme cases of the stress distribution found in lateral spread can cause the sheet to split down the center (Fig. 17-10c).
*   **Alligatoring:**
    *   *Description:* The workpiece splits along a horizontal plane at the center, with the upper and lower halves curling outward like an alligator's mouth (Fig. 17-11c).
    *   *Cause:* Secondary tensile stresses along the centerline, often aggravated by metallurgical weakness (like inclusions) or if the sheet curls due to uneven roll speeds.

### 4. Metallurgical and Surface Defects
These originate from the ingot stage or the rolling practice itself.

*   **Laminations:** Caused by internal defects such as pipe, blowholes, or nonmetallic inclusions (stringers) that are flattened during rolling. They drastically reduce strength in the thickness direction.
*   **Surface Defects:**
    *   **Slivers, Seams, and Scabs:** Result from defects on the ingot surface that were not removed by conditioning (grinding or chipping) before rolling.
    *   **Scratches:** Caused by defective rolls or guides.
    *   **Orange Peel:** A surface roughening caused by heavy deformation in sheet metal with a large grain size.
